    A study in Sri Lanka reveals high vehicle speeds, small-bodied animals, and nighttime driving are major factors contributing to animal-vehicle collisions (AVCs). AVCs result in 161 accidents annually, killing 40 species of animals.The study utilized road surveys, driver surveys, and interviews with wildlife experts to identify drivers' main causes of AVCs. They believed high vehicle speeds were the main cause, with small-bodied animals more likely to be hit. Nighttime visibility made it difficult for drivers to identify animals, potentially causing accidents. The study highlights the importance of understanding the causes and factors of AVCs to develop strategies for wildlife conservation and road safety [1]. Measures to reduce AVCs in Sri Lanka include reducing vehicle speeds, raising driver awareness, installing wildlife crossing signs, and educating the public about the importance of wildlife conservation.

    Research Problem & Solution

    Proposed Problem

    Animal-vehicle collisions (AVCs) in Sri Lanka pose a significant threat to biodiversity, human safety, and economic costs. These collisions occur due to the close interaction between human and wildlife habitats, resulting in an estimated 26,489 motor vehicle and animal accidents annually. The lack of public awareness and negligence towards AVCs exacerbates the problem, leading to a lack of effective mitigation measures. Road networks often fragment animal habitats and home ranges, leading to a reduction in animal population size. To reduce AVCs, vehicles must be properly identified and addressed. Conventional warning signs, wildlife warning reflectors, or fences have limited effectiveness, as drivers may become accustomed to them. Wildlife fencing has been combined with wildlife crossing structures in some areas. To effectively address AVCs, a comprehensive and integrated approach must be implemented, including installing a wildlife detection system, identifying animal habitats for preservation and restoration, improving vehicle technology, driver education, and implementing wildlife monitoring programs.

    High Level Architecture of the system

    What We Provide !s

    The proposed animal and vehicle Surveillance system consists of 5 main components. They are;

    • Geo Fencing Stratergy with rowdsourcing for information sharing
    • Seen Identification
    • Unseen Identification
    • Overall Solution

    The primary focus will be on conducting a knowledge based case study to determine the habitat and behavior of animals in various locations . This study will involve gathering and retrieving data to inform passengers, utilizing crowd-sourcing technologies to obtain additional details and insights from people. Additionally, an RSS feed will be used to obtain news and weather information for specific areas, enabling the notification of users in different locations when necessary. The second aspect of the study aims to capture animals from moving vehicles, identifying their behaviors, and conveying information to users. It will focus on clear, high quality footage of different animal types, enabling identification of direction and distance. Computer vision and image processing techniques will be employed to overcome human observation limitations and improve animal detection. The third aspect, Unseen identification is highly effective in dark environments , as a signal is transmitted to the environment and processed to identify animals within a specified distance. Research has focused on frequency, frequency emission, and rollback frequency recovery after being hit by animals. Key factors include vehicle vibration, Doppler effect, moving object laws, and time. The study proposes improved solutions for animal vehicle collision mitigation, including guidelines for implementing techniques and public education. It involves projecting anti-frequency waves onto animals and providing instructions for responding in encountered scenarios. User feedback and experience are crucial, aiming to prevent conflicts and minimize tragic incidents

    Research Publication

    IoT-Enhanced Smart Surveillance System for Wildlife Collision Prevention on Sri Lankan Roads

    This research paper presents a mobileapplication-based solution for real-time road safetyaimed at mitigating animal-vehicle conflicts in SriLanka. The proposed system is developed in response tothe increasing incidents of animal-vehicle collisions,which pose significant risks to both human safety andwildlife conservation efforts. By combining knowledge-based case studies and crowd-sourcing techniques, theapplication aims to identify animal habitats andbehaviors based on user location, enabling drivers totake proactive measures to prevent such accidents.Image processing is used to identify objects usingYOLOv7 technology. Ultrasonic sensors, amicrocontroller, the Doppler Effect, and relative velocitycalculations are used to segment the signal-to-noise ratioand alert the driver of any nearby animals. The researchalso provides guidelines for implementing methods toreduce animal-vehicle collisions and raises awarenessamong the public regarding the importance of roadsafety in wildlife-rich areas. Upon detection of theanimal, the system emits an anti-frequency rangetailored to specific species, effectively deterring themfrom approaching the vehicle. Through the mobileapplication, users can access real-time alerts, receivefeedback from others, and contribute to a collectiveeffort in ensuring safer roads for both humans andanimals.

    Frequently Asked Questioins

    • What are the steps drivers can take to reduce the risk of animal-vehicle collisions?

      Pay close attention to the road and your surroundings, especially in areas known for wildlife crossings.

      Avoid distractions, such as texting or using your phone while driving.

      Follow posted speed limits, and reduce your speed in areas where wildlife crossing signs are present..

      Slower speeds can give you more time to react if an animal suddenly appears on the road.

    • What are the most common types of animals involved in vehicle collisions in Sri Lanka?

      Elephants: Sri Lanka is known for its population of wild elephants, and they often wander onto roads and highways, especially in rural and forested areas. Collisions with elephants can be particularly dangerous and damaging.

      Deer: Various species of deer, such as sambar deer and axis deer, are common in Sri Lankan forests and can frequently be seen near roadways. They can dart onto roads unexpectedly, leading to collisions.

      Wild boars: Wild boars are also prevalent in many parts of Sri Lanka. They are known to cross roads and can pose a collision risk, especially at night

    • What are the main reasons for animal-vehicle collisions in Sri Lanka?

      Habitat fragmentation: As human development expands and roads are built through previously undisturbed habitats, animals are often forced to cross roads to access food, water, or shelter. This increases the chances of collisions.

      Lack of wildlife corridors: In many areas, there are inadequate wildlife corridors or underpasses/overpasses designed to help animals safely cross roads. This lack of infrastructure makes it more likely for animals to come into direct contact with vehicles.

      Nighttime driving: Many animals, such as deer, are more active at dawn and dusk or during the nighttime hours. Reduced visibility during nighttime driving can lead to an increased risk of collisions with these nocturnal animals.

    • Are there any measures in place to prevent animal-vehicle collisions?

      Wildlife corridors: Creating wildlife corridors or overpasses/underpasses at strategic locations along roads can provide safe passages for animals to cross without coming into direct contact with vehicles. These corridors are often designed based on known animal movement patterns.

      Fencing: Wildlife fencing, such as tall barriers along roadsides, can deter animals from entering roadways. Fencing is especially effective in areas with high wildlife traffic.

      Warning signs and speed limits: Road authorities often place warning signs to alert drivers to the presence of wildlife and advise reduced speeds in areas known for animal crossings. These signs can serve as reminders for drivers to exercise caution.

    • Are there any innovative technologies or methods being introduced or developed to reduce the incidence of animal-vehicle collisions?

      Wildlife detection systems: These systems use various technologies, including thermal imaging, infrared cameras, and radar, to detect the presence of animals near roads. When an animal is detected, warning signs can be activated to alert drivers to the potential danger. Some systems can also automatically trigger road-side lighting to increase visibility.

      Animal-activated warning systems: In certain locations, pressure-sensitive mats or sensors are installed near roads. When an animal approaches the road, these sensors can trigger flashing lights or audible warnings to alert drivers.

      Animal-resistant fencing: In areas with high wildlife activity, specially designed animal-resistant fencing can be installed to deter animals from entering roadways. These fences are typically taller and stronger than conventional road barriers.

    • How effective are warning signs , systems and speed limits in reducing animal-vehicle collisions?

      Warning signs that indicate the presence of wildlife and suggest reduced speeds can be effective in alerting drivers to the potential danger. Drivers who are aware of the risks are more likely to exercise caution.

      The effectiveness of warning signs can be influenced by driver compliance. If drivers ignore or disregard the signs, their impact is diminished. Education and public awareness campaigns can help improve compliance.

      Warning signs can be especially helpful in areas with known wildlife crossing patterns or during specific seasons when animal activity is higher.
